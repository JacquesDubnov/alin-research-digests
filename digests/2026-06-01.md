### Reproductive History, Hormonal Exposure, and Risk of Heart Failure in Postmenopausal Women
- **Authors:** Kim LY et al.
- **Journal:** European Heart Journal, 2026
- **PMID:** 42178981
- **DOI:** [10.1093/eurheartj/ehag380](https://doi.org/10.1093/eurheartj/ehag380)
- **Domain:** Query 4 — Cardiovascular/MHT
- **Relevance to ALIN:** Provides strong reassurance data on MHT and cardiac outcomes in a massive real-world dataset — directly useful for ALIN's content on cardiovascular benefits of sustained MHT.

> In 3,692,157 postmenopausal Korean women, MHT use ≥5 years was associated with a 21.8% lower risk of heart failure hospitalization (SHR 0.782, 99.3% CI 0.708–0.863) compared with non-users. Oral contraceptive use was also protective. Earlier age at menopause and shorter reproductive period were associated with higher HF risk and mortality. Prolonged lifetime exposure to both endogenous and exogenous female sex hormones correlated with better cardiac outcomes.

### Health Outcomes of Hormone Therapy Initiated or Continued After Age 65
- **Authors:** Carney A et al.
- **Journal:** Menopause, 2026
- **PMID:** 41632236
- **DOI:** [10.1097/GME.0000000000002721](https://doi.org/10.1097/GME.0000000000002721)
- **Domain:** Query 1 — HRT safety/timing
- **Relevance to ALIN:** Reinforces the timing hypothesis for ALIN users considering late initiation; important for counseling women who contact ALIN post-65.

> Retrospective cohort of 83,147 women (Clalit Health Services, 2000–2022). Initiation of HT at ≥65 years was associated with increased hazard of any cancer (HR 2.22) and cerebrovascular accident (HR 2.70) compared with never-users. HT initiated at 50–65 years showed markedly elevated crude cardiovascular advantage but this was not retained in adjusted Cox models. Authors conclude that late HT initiation should involve individualized risk-benefit assessment and close monitoring.

### Evaluation of Breast Risk at Menopause: What Impact on the Use of Menopausal Hormone Therapy?
- **Authors:** Rodrigues MAH, Gompel A
- **Journal:** Maturitas, 2026
- **PMID:** 42202686
- **DOI:** [10.1016/j.maturitas.2026.108998](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.maturitas.2026.108998)
- **Domain:** Query 1 — Breast cancer risk/MHT
- **Relevance to ALIN:** Practical framework for how ALIN can help users understand individualized breast cancer risk before considering MHT; highlights which risk scoring tools are most used clinically.

> Review covering breast cancer risk stratification tools (IBIS score, Gail score, CANrisk) for guiding MHT decisions. Even with low-risk progestogens (micronized progesterone, dydrogesterone), a slight increase in breast cancer risk is seen after ~6 years of MHT — consistent with a promoter effect on pre-existing lesions. Very high-risk patients (BRCA carriers, atypical hyperplasia, prior thoracic radiotherapy, high breast density) should consider non-hormonal alternatives.

### Association Between Hormone Replacement Therapy and Incidence of Tendon Injuries and Surgical Repair in Perimenopausal Women
- **Authors:** Bcharah G et al.
- **Journal:** Orthopaedic Journal of Sports Medicine, 2026
- **PMID:** 42212202
- **DOI:** [10.1177/23259671261428457](https://doi.org/10.1177/23259671261428457)
- **Domain:** Query 1 — Musculoskeletal effects of HRT
- **Relevance to ALIN:** Highlights an underexplored area of MHT risks — tendon health — that ALIN users asking about HRT side effects may not encounter elsewhere.

> National cohort study using TriNetX (63,836 propensity-matched pairs). HRT users had significantly higher rates of rotator cuff tears (39.2% vs 37.9%), lateral epicondylitis (27.8% vs 22.7%), and medial epicondylitis (6.8% vs 5.3%) vs matched controls. Surgical repair was also more frequent in HRT users. Absolute risk differences were modest; authors note this underexplored musculoskeletal dimension of HRT warrants clinical awareness.

### Menopause and Vascular Endothelial Health: Is It All About the Oestrogen?
- **Authors:** Nuckols VR et al.
- **Journal:** Experimental Physiology, 2026
- **PMID:** 42218087
- **DOI:** [10.1113/EP092848](https://doi.org/10.1113/EP092848)
- **Domain:** Query 4/5 — Cardiovascular/sex hormones
- **Relevance to ALIN:** Directly challenges the estrogen-only narrative of menopausal cardiovascular risk, supporting ALIN's nuanced explanation of the menopausal transition.

> Review synthesizing clinical and translational evidence on how progesterone decline, rising FSH and LH, and testosterone changes — not just estradiol loss — affect vascular endothelial function through endothelin-1 signalling, oxidative stress, and inflammatory pathways. Argues that attributing all cardiovascular risk acceleration to estradiol decline alone is an oversimplification.

### Tailoring Transdermal Estradiol Dose to Maximize Benefits and Minimize Risks
- **Authors:** Glynne SJ, Simon JA
- **Journal:** Menopause, 2026
- **PMID:** 41529140
- **DOI:** [10.1097/GME.0000000000002723](https://doi.org/10.1097/GME.0000000000002723)
- **Domain:** Query 1 — HRT optimization
- **Relevance to ALIN:** Supports ALIN's messaging on personalized MHT: no universal dose exists, and serum estradiol monitoring can guide dose titration in certain scenarios.

> Review covering transdermal estradiol pharmacokinetics and pharmacodynamics, noting that no one-size-fits-all dose or serum concentration achieves symptom relief and bone protection in all women. Optimal estradiol levels vary by tissue sensitivity, measurement method (immunoassay vs mass spectrometry), and clinical endpoint. Dose titration to symptom response is first-line; serum levels inform decisions in complex clinical scenarios.

### Climacteric Symptoms Among Full-Time Working HRT Never-Users in Different Work Environments
- **Authors:** Lipasti M et al.
- **Journal:** Journal of Obstetrics and Gynaecology, 2026
- **PMID:** 42201899
- **DOI:** [10.1080/01443615.2026.2678186](https://doi.org/10.1080/01443615.2026.2678186)
- **Domain:** Query 1 — Symptom burden/occupational health
- **Relevance to ALIN:** Quantifies the symptom burden in untreated working women and the compounding effect of job strain — directly relevant to ALIN's working-women user persona.

> Cross-sectional study of 338 untreated Finnish women (age 52–66). Hot flushes (80%), sweats (72%), and sleep problems (72%) were most prevalent; each was rated "severe" by 12% of participants. Women in high-strain jobs experienced significantly more symptoms overall (p=0.019), more urinary incontinence (p=0.008), more depressive symptoms (p=0.043), and more severe sweating (p=0.038) than those in low-strain jobs.

### "There Is Absolutely No Facet of My Life That It Hasn't Affected" — Experiences of Symptoms During Perimenopause
- **Authors:** Murphy M et al.
- **Journal:** Journal of Women & Aging, 2026
- **PMID:** 42212519
- **DOI:** [10.1080/08952841.2026.2680873](https://doi.org/10.1080/08952841.2026.2680873)
- **Domain:** Query 2 — Patient experience/perimenopause
- **Relevance to ALIN:** Qualitative evidence of the multidimensional, poorly-anticipated impact of perimenopause; validates ALIN's empathetic, personalized approach and the demand for peer support.

> Qualitative study of 17 late-stage perimenopausal Irish women. Four themes: (1) being ill-prepared for perimenopause onset; (2) the sweeping impact of symptoms on all life domains; (3) active information and support seeking; (4) using this phase for introspective reflection. Peer support was highly valued; women emphasized the need for sensitivity from medical professionals and individualized guidance.

### Primary Care Provider Practice Patterns in the Management of Recurrent UTI
- **Authors:** Tholemeier LN et al.
- **Journal:** Urogynecology, 2026
- **PMID:** 41811789
- **DOI:** [10.1097/SPV.0000000000001719](https://doi.org/10.1097/SPV.0000000000001719)
- **Domain:** Query 5 — GSM/genitourinary syndrome of menopause
- **Relevance to ALIN:** Reveals how poorly vaginal estrogen is utilized for recurrent UTI prevention in postmenopausal women — a gap ALIN can help users advocate to close with their providers.

> Survey of 50 primary care providers. Only 62% would consider vaginal estrogen as first option for recurrent UTI prophylaxis. A concerning 28% would recommend long-term antibiotic prophylaxis even for uncomplicated cases. Non-antibiotic alternatives (methenamine hippurate 26%, D-mannose 17%) were underused. Providers with "focused" menopause training were more comfortable prescribing vaginal estrogen across clinical scenarios.

### Co-Designing a Culturally Appropriate mHealth Physical Activity Intervention for Midlife Women Experiencing Menopause in Saudi Arabia
- **Authors:** AlSwayied G et al.
- **Journal:** BMC Public Health, 2026
- **PMID:** 42204502
- **DOI:** [10.1186/s12889-026-27624-6](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12889-026-27624-6)
- **Domain:** Query 2 — Digital health/mHealth for menopause
- **Relevance to ALIN:** Directly applicable co-design methodology for a digital menopause health tool; lessons on cultural tailoring, empathetic framing of PA as self-care, and ecosystem integration apply to ALIN's design.

> Multi-stakeholder co-design workshops (n=23) with Saudi midlife women, HCPs, policymakers, and fitness trainers. ### Sex Hormone Levels and Sarcopenia Risk Among Older Adults Over 65 in China
- **Authors:** Li ZP et al.
- **Journal:** Chinese Journal of Preventive Medicine, 2026
- **PMID:** 42206488
- **DOI:** [10.3760/cma.j.cn112150-20251217-01192](https://doi.org/10.3760/cma.j.cn112150-20251217-01192)
- **Domain:** Query 5 — Testosterone/hormones and musculoskeletal
- **Relevance to ALIN:** Supports ALIN's content on testosterone's role beyond libido — here showing a meaningful protective effect against sarcopenia in postmenopausal women.

> Cross-sectional study of 4,695 older adults (HABCS 2021 survey; n=2,455 women). In postmenopausal women, high-testosterone group had 33% lower sarcopenia risk (OR 0.67, 95% CI 0.48–0.92) vs the medium group. Elevated FSH was associated with 21% higher sarcopenia risk per 1-SD increment (OR 1.21, 95% CI 1.05–1.40). Overall sarcopenia prevalence was 27.8%.

### Associations Between Physical Activity, Sleep Patterns, and Diet Quality and Menstrual Health Symptoms in Midlife
- **Authors:** Abdullayev K et al.
- **Journal:** BMC Women's Health, 2026
- **PMID:** 42192416
- **DOI:** [10.1186/s12905-026-04533-9](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12905-026-04533-9)
- **Domain:** Query 2 — Lifestyle/perimenopause
- **Relevance to ALIN:** Provides lifestyle modification evidence for perimenopausal symptom management, specifically linking sleep regularity (not just duration) to multiple menstrual symptoms.

> Longitudinal data from 2,109 women in the 1970 British Cohort Study (age 46 sweep). Lowest quartile of moderate-vigorous PA had 1.35× higher odds of painful periods. Lowest sleep regularity had 1.43× higher odds of painful periods, 1.34× heavy periods, and 1.33× PMS. Sleep duration was not associated with menstrual outcomes — regularity mattered more than total hours.
