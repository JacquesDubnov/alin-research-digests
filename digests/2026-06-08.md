### Potential Risk of Neoplasms With Fezolinetant: Clinical Evidence, Mechanisms, and Post-Marketing Implications
- **Authors:** Boretti E et al.
- **Journal:** Journal of the Endocrine Society, 2026
- **PMID:** 42137787
- **Domain:** Query 3 — NK3 antagonist / non-hormonal VMS
- **Relevance to ALIN:** Critical safety signal for a drug ALIN users may be prescribed; fezolinetant's non-hormonal appeal is now complicated by an emerging neoplasm signal that ALIN must communicate carefully and accurately.

> In 2023 the FDA/EMA approved fezolinetant (Veozah) as the first nonhormonal NK3R antagonist for moderate-to-severe menopausal VMS. However, emerging data from pooled analyses and meta-analyses of the SKYLIGHT and MOONLIGHT programs consistently demonstrate a statistically significant increase in the risk of nonbenign neoplasms in fezolinetant-treated patients vs. placebo. The paper reviews proposed mechanisms, regulatory implications, and calls for enhanced post-marketing surveillance.

### The Effect of Hormone Therapy on Vaginal Microbiota in Women With Genitourinary Syndrome of Menopause: A Double-Blind, Randomized, Placebo-Controlled Trial
- **Authors:** Panyakhamlerd K et al.
- **Journal:** Maturitas, 2026
- **PMID:** 42097107
- **Domain:** Query 1 — HRT / Query 5 — GSM
- **Relevance to ALIN:** First RCT to characterize the vaginal microbiome impact of oral systemic MHT in GSM — directly expands ALIN's content on why MHT helps beyond symptom relief, connecting hormonal therapy to microbiota restoration.

> Double-blind RCT of 34 postmenopausal women with moderate-to-severe GSM randomized to oral estradiol 1 mg/dydrogesterone 5 mg or placebo for 12 weeks. Microbiota was assessed by 16S rDNA sequencing. MHT significantly increased relative abundance of Lactobacillus spp. and improved vaginal maturation index, pH, and symptom scores vs. placebo, suggesting systemic MHT partially restores the healthy lactobacillus-dominant vaginal microbiome lost at menopause.

### Comparison of Two Menopausal Hormone Therapy Regimens in Managing Menopausal Symptoms: A Retrospective Study
- **Authors:** Yu Y et al.
- **Journal:** BMC Women's Health, 2026
- **PMID:** 42226203
- **Domain:** Query 1 — HRT / Query 2 — Perimenopause treatment
- **Relevance to ALIN:** Rare head-to-head data comparing two commonly prescribed sequential MHT regimens; actionable for ALIN users choosing between dydrogesterone- vs. cyproterone acetate-based therapy.

> Retrospective study of 80 peri- and early postmenopausal women comparing Femoston (estradiol/dydrogesterone) vs. Climen (estradiol valerate/cyproterone acetate). Both regimens effectively reduced Kupperman Index scores and improved lipid profiles. Femoston showed marginally greater improvement in hot flash frequency; Climen showed a more pronounced effect on androgenic symptoms such as acne and hirsutism. Endometrial safety was similar between groups.

### Menopausal Hormone Therapy and Pulmonary Diseases
- **Authors:** Hipólito Rodrigues MA et al.
- **Journal:** Climacteric, 2026
- **PMID:** 42234514
- **Domain:** Query 1 — HRT safety/systemic effects
- **Relevance to ALIN:** Underexplored MHT safety domain; important for users with asthma, COPD, or other pulmonary conditions who ask whether MHT is safe for them.

> Narrative review on how sex hormones (estrogen, progesterone, androgens) influence lung development, airway smooth muscle tone, immune modulation, and vascular remodeling. Menopause-related hormone loss accelerates airway decline in women with asthma and COPD. Transdermal MHT appears to improve pulmonary function and reduce exacerbation frequency in observational data; risk-benefit analysis is warranted in women with comorbid pulmonary disease.

### Migraine and Stroke Risk in Postmenopausal Women in the Women's Health Initiative
- **Authors:** Madsen TE et al.
- **Journal:** Neurology, 2026
- **PMID:** 42247650
- **Domain:** Query 4 — Cardiovascular risk / cognitive
- **Relevance to ALIN:** Clarifies whether the well-known migraine–stroke link in younger women persists postmenopause; directly affects how ALIN discusses stroke risk for users with migraine history.

> WHI longitudinal cohort study examining migraine history and incident stroke in postmenopausal women. Women with a history of migraine had a significantly elevated risk of ischemic stroke compared with non-migraine controls, with the association persisting after adjustment for traditional cardiovascular risk factors. The study calls for active cardiovascular risk management in postmenopausal women with migraine, not just in younger women.

### Migraine and Menopause as Converging Factors in Brain Vulnerability: A Hypothesis-Driven Perspective
- **Authors:** Gazerani P et al.
- **Journal:** The Journal of Headache and Pain, 2026
- **PMID:** 42243649
- **Domain:** Query 4 — Cognitive / neurological
- **Relevance to ALIN:** Provides the mechanistic framework linking migraine and menopause to accelerated brain aging — useful for ALIN's brain health content pillar, especially for users with both conditions.

> Narrative review synthesizing neuroimaging, clinical, and neuroendocrine evidence on the migraine–menopause interaction. Estrogen withdrawal during the menopausal transition amplifies cortical spreading depression susceptibility and neuroinflammatory pathways already dysregulated in migraine. ### Physiologic Changes in Sleep Across the Female Lifespan
- **Authors:** Tripipitsiriwat A et al.
- **Journal:** Sleep Medicine Clinics, 2026
- **PMID:** 42161450
- **Domain:** Query 4 — Sleep / hormonal transition
- **Relevance to ALIN:** Foundational reference for ALIN's sleep content pillar — establishes how estrogen and progesterone directly modulate sleep architecture and risk of sleep disorders at every reproductive life stage.

> Comprehensive review of how ovarian hormone fluctuations alter sleep from menarche through postmenopause. Estrogen promotes REM sleep and circadian rhythm stability; progesterone has sedating properties and reduces apnea severity. Menopausal transition disrupts both, increasing insomnia, sleep fragmentation, and obstructive sleep apnea risk by up to 3-fold postmenopause. Both direct (hormonal) and indirect (thermoregulatory, mood) effects are detailed.

### Sleep Duration and Mortality in Women: Effect of Aging and Menopause
- **Authors:** Ji M et al.
- **Journal:** Sleep Medicine Clinics, 2026
- **PMID:** 42161456
- **Domain:** Query 4 — Sleep / mortality
- **Relevance to ALIN:** Strengthens ALIN's messaging on sleep as a longevity factor, specifically contextualizing optimal sleep within the menopause experience and showing race/ethnicity differences.

> Review establishing a U-shaped relationship between nightly sleep duration and all-cause mortality in women; 7–8 hours is associated with lowest risk. Menopause transition shifts this curve, increasing mortality associated with short sleep (<6 h). Race/ethnicity modifies risk: Black women show higher mortality at short sleep durations than white women with equivalent hours. Age-related changes in sleep architecture, OSA risk, and menopausal status compound the mortality signal.

### Estrogen Prevents Menopausal Obesity
- **Authors:** Maturana MA et al.
- **Journal:** Menopause, 2026
- **PMID:** 42228467
- **Domain:** Query 4 — Metabolic health / MHT
- **Relevance to ALIN:** Directly supports ALIN's messaging on weight management at menopause; makes the case that estrogen therapy is an evidence-based metabolic intervention, not just a symptom-relief treatment.

> Review defining "menopausal obesity" and "high-risk menopausal obesity (HRMO)" as phenotypes of visceral adiposity accumulation driven by estrogen loss at menopause. Estrogen therapy reduces visceral fat, improves insulin sensitivity, and prevents the hepatic lipid accumulation that underpins HRMO. Weight control and MHT timing (before metabolic syndrome establishes) are identified as the key modifiable factors preventing HRMO and its downstream cardiovascular complications.

### Optimizing Women's Cardiovascular Health Across the Life Course: The Role of Physical Activity in Female-Specific Conditions
- **Authors:** Le Bourvellec M et al.
- **Journal:** Archives of Cardiovascular Diseases, 2026
- **PMID:** 42161737
- **Domain:** Query 4 — Cardiovascular health / lifestyle
- **Relevance to ALIN:** Provides consolidated evidence for physical activity prescriptions ALIN can tailor to each life stage, including menopause-specific cardiovascular risks and exercise-dose recommendations.

> Narrative review across five female-specific cardiovascular risk domains: hormonal contraception, PCOS, endometriosis, pregnancy complications (preeclampsia/GDM), and menopause. For menopause specifically, moderate-to-vigorous aerobic exercise ≥150 min/week preserves endothelial function, reduces visceral fat, improves lipid profiles, and attenuates postmenopausal blood pressure elevation. Resistance training additionally protects bone and muscle mass. Both aerobic and resistance modalities are endorsed.

### The Effect of Vaginal Tamoxifen on Symptoms of Anxiety, Depression, and Health-Related Quality of Life in Postmenopausal Women With and Without Breast Cancer
- **Authors:** Yazici Sarikaya S et al.
- **Journal:** Maturitas, 2026
- **PMID:** 42235095
- **Domain:** Query 5 — GSM / non-estrogen alternatives
- **Relevance to ALIN:** Expands ALIN's non-estrogen GSM treatment options, particularly for users with breast cancer history or estrogen contraindications who are struggling with both GSM and psychological symptoms.

> Prospective study evaluating vaginal tamoxifen as a SERM-based local treatment for GSM in postmenopausal women with and without breast cancer. Vaginal tamoxifen significantly reduced GSM symptom scores and improved anxiety (GAD-7), depression (PHQ-9), and health-related QoL (MENQOL) scores after 12 weeks. No endometrial stimulation was observed. Authors propose vaginal tamoxifen as a promising estrogen-free option for GSM-related psychological burden.

### Clinical and Vaginal Microbiota Effects of Oral Lactobacillus crispatus M247 Combined With Vaginal Laser Therapy in Menopausal Women With Atrophic Vulvovaginitis
- **Authors:** Di Pierro F et al.
- **Journal:** Lasers in Medical Science, 2026
- **PMID:** 42234022
- **Domain:** Query 5 — GSM / vaginal microbiome
- **Relevance to ALIN:** Supports ALIN's evidence-based approach to GSM with non-hormonal or adjunct options; demonstrates microbiota-targeted therapies as a meaningful complement to laser procedures.

> RCT comparing vaginal CO2 laser therapy alone vs. laser plus daily oral Lactobacillus crispatus M247 probiotic in postmenopausal women with atrophic vulvovaginitis. The combination arm showed significantly greater improvement in vaginal dryness, dyspareunia, and pH normalization vs. laser alone. 16S sequencing confirmed restoration of Lactobacillus-dominant microbiota profiles in the combination group but not laser alone. No adverse effects from the probiotic were observed.

### Androgen Therapy and Musculoskeletal Health in Older Adults: An Umbrella Review
- **Authors:** López Hilario PP et al.
- **Journal:** Semergen, 2026
- **PMID:** 42235305
- **Domain:** Query 5 — Testosterone / musculoskeletal
- **Relevance to ALIN:** Synthesizes the highest-level evidence on testosterone's musculoskeletal benefits beyond libido — key for ALIN's content on testosterone's expanding indications in perimenopausal and postmenopausal women.

> Umbrella review of systematic reviews and meta-analyses of RCTs on androgen supplementation and musculoskeletal outcomes in adults over 50. Testosterone supplementation consistently improved lean muscle mass and grip strength in postmenopausal women; effects on bone mineral density were less consistent. Adverse effect profile (acne, androgenic alopecia) was dose-dependent and generally mild. Authors support individualized androgen therapy within established safety guidelines.

### Insights Into Lower Reproductive Tract Aging: Mechanisms and Perspectives
- **Authors:** Wang R et al.
- **Journal:** Mechanisms of Ageing and Development, 2026
- **PMID:** 42128097
- **Domain:** Query 5 — GSM / vaginal aging
- **Relevance to ALIN:** Provides the cellular and molecular biology underpinning GSM for ALIN's science-based content; helps explain *why* GSM symptoms worsen progressively and why early treatment matters.

> Comprehensive mechanistic review of lower reproductive tract aging (uterus, cervix, vagina) driven by postmenopausal estrogen depletion. Key pathways: chronic low-grade inflammation, mitochondrial dysfunction, cellular senescence (SASP), and oxidative stress. These converge to produce endometrial atrophy, vaginal epithelial thinning, disrupted collagen architecture, and microbiome dysbiosis — the biological substrate of GSM. Authors highlight local estrogen and emerging senolytic therapies as targeted interventions.

### Navigating the Challenges of Overactive Bladder Management in Women
- **Authors:** Braga A et al.
- **Journal:** World Journal of Urology, 2026
- **PMID:** 42223703
- **Domain:** Query 5 — GSM / lower urinary tract
- **Relevance to ALIN:** OAB is among the most common and undertreated menopausal complaints; this IUGA/EUGA consensus-derived review provides current guidance ALIN can translate for users who conflate OAB with GSM.

> Narrative review from an IUGA/EUGA symposium addressing OAB management uniquely in women. Covers the interplay of menopausal estrogen loss with urethral and detrusor muscle function; distinguishes OAB from stress incontinence and GSM-driven urgency. Reviews behavioral (bladder training, pelvic floor therapy), pharmacological (antimuscarinics, β3-agonists), and procedural (botulinum toxin, PTNS) options with a sex-specific lens. Highlights the role of vaginal estrogen in reducing recurrent UTI and improving treatment response.

### Is There Evidence of Diagnostic and Treatment Gaps for LUTS in Post-Menopausal Women With Genitourinary Syndrome of the Menopause? ICI-RS 2025
- **Authors:** Apostolidis A et al.
- **Journal:** Neurourology and Urodynamics, 2025
- **PMID:** 41293843
- **Domain:** Query 5 — GSM / LUTS / clinical gaps
- **Relevance to ALIN:** ICI consensus report identifying the exact diagnostic and treatment gaps ALIN is positioned to help fill — women with GSM-related LUTS are underdiagnosed and undertreated.

> International Consultation on Incontinence Research Society 2025 report synthesizing evidence on LUTS burden in postmenopausal women with GSM. Major gaps identified: (1) clinicians rarely screen for GSM as a LUTS driver; (2) vaginal estrogen is underutilized for recurrent UTI prevention; (3) underactive bladder in postmenopausal women remains poorly characterized. Recommendations include GSM screening in all women presenting with LUTS and standardized outcome measures for GSM-LUTS trials.

### Prevalence of Vasomotor Symptoms and the Associated Risk Factors: The "Mi Menopausia" App Study
- **Authors:** Coronado PJ et al.
- **Journal:** Maturitas, 2026
- **PMID:** 42066667
- **Domain:** Query 2 — Perimenopause / VMS epidemiology
- **Relevance to ALIN:** The largest Spanish-language real-world VMS dataset to date, providing demographic and lifestyle risk factor data highly relevant to ALIN's Spanish-speaking user base.

> Cross-sectional study of 11,378 peri- and postmenopausal Spanish women using the Mi Menopausia app (June 2021–November 2025). VMS prevalence was 78.3%; moderate-to-severe VMS affected 44.1%. Risk factors associated with greater VMS burden: higher BMI, smoking, sedentary lifestyle, surgical menopause, lower educational level, and depression history. VMS significantly reduced health-related QoL across all MENQOL domains. The app-based recruitment enabled rapid large-scale real-world phenotyping.

### Cognitive-Behavioral Interventions for (Peri)Menopausal Symptoms: A Scoping Review of Intervention Characteristics and Content
- **Authors:** Branquinho M et al.
- **Journal:** Patient Education and Counseling, 2026
- **PMID:** 41713169
- **Domain:** Query 2 — Perimenopause / non-hormonal / CBT
- **Relevance to ALIN:** Directly informs ALIN's behavioral health module design; establishes which CBT components and delivery formats have evidence for which menopausal symptoms.

> Scoping review of 38 studies on CBT-based interventions for menopausal symptoms. Most effective formats: individual or group sessions (6–8 sessions, 60–90 min each) combining psychoeducation, cognitive restructuring, and behavioral strategies for hot flashes (thought challenging, cooling techniques), sleep (stimulus control, sleep restriction), and mood (behavioral activation). Digital and group delivery formats showed comparable efficacy to face-to-face. Authors call for standardized outcome measures to enable cross-study synthesis.

### The Effectiveness of Lifestyle Interventions, Including Exercise, Diet, and Health Education on Symptoms Experienced During Perimenopause: A Systematic Review of Randomized Controlled Trials
- **Authors:** McNulty KL et al.
- **Journal:** Journal of Aging and Physical Activity, 2025
- **PMID:** 40992413
- **Domain:** Query 2 — Perimenopause / lifestyle
- **Relevance to ALIN:** Systematic review providing the evidence base for ALIN's lifestyle recommendation engine; helps ALIN identify which interventions have RCT support and for which symptoms.

> Systematic review of 24 RCTs examining exercise, dietary, and health education interventions for perimenopausal symptoms. Exercise (aerobic + resistance) showed the most consistent benefits across VMS frequency, sleep quality, mood, and QoL. Diet modifications (Mediterranean-style, low-fat) showed modest benefits for VMS and metabolic health. Health education alone improved coping and QoL but not physiological symptom burden. Evidence quality was generally moderate; heterogeneous outcomes limit pooled conclusions.

### Paradigm Shift in Nonhormonal Treatment Options for Vasomotor Symptoms of Menopause
- **Authors:** Flanagan MR et al.
- **Journal:** Nursing for Women's Health, 2026
- **PMID:** 41997569
- **Domain:** Query 3 — Non-hormonal VMS treatment
- **Relevance to ALIN:** Clinician-facing review ideal for ALIN's content library on why the treatment landscape for VMS has shifted; helps users understand their non-hormonal options beyond SSRIs.

> Review covering the evolution of nonhormonal VMS management, with focus on NK3R antagonists (fezolinetant, elinzanetant) as the first approved mechanism-targeted nonhormonal options. Also covers SSRIs/SNRIs (paroxetine, escitalopram, venlafaxine), gabapentin, oxybutynin, and clonidine with updated efficacy and safety data. Authors emphasize that up to 80% of menopausal people experience VMS, yet many cannot or prefer not to use hormones — creating urgent clinical demand for effective alternatives.

### Effects of a High-Intensity Functional Exercise Program on Menopausal Symptoms, Physical Performance, and Dual-Task Performance in Postmenopausal Women: A Randomized Controlled Trial
- **Authors:** Gözütok NH et al.
- **Journal:** BMC Women's Health, 2026
- **PMID:** 42226147
- **Domain:** Query 2 — Exercise / postmenopause
- **Relevance to ALIN:** Provides RCT-level evidence for high-intensity exercise in postmenopausal women — directly supports ALIN's exercise recommendations for users who want more than gentle yoga.

> RCT of 40 postmenopausal women (ages 50–65) randomized to an 8-week high-intensity functional exercise (HIFE) program vs. control. HIFE significantly reduced Menopause Rating Scale total scores (particularly psychological and somatic subscales), improved 30-second chair-stand test, timed up-and-go, and dual-task walking performance vs. controls. Authors conclude HIFE is safe, feasible, and effective for comprehensive menopausal symptom management in previously inactive postmenopausal women.
